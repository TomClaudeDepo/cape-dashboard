01 / Structural trend

The rising global middle class

The single most powerful structural tailwind for travel demand. Over 100 million people join the consumer class every year — and travel is the first "luxury" they reach for.

Global middle class population

Billions of people, 2020–2035 projected

Middle class spending by segment

Trillions USD, stacked by income tier

The World Economic Forum reports that the global middle and rich classes now total 4.4 billion people, outnumbering the 3.6 billion poor for the first time in human history. They collectively spend over $60 trillion annually. In 2025 alone, 106 million individuals are projected to join the consumer class — and despite being the slowest expansion since the pandemic, another billion are expected within the next decade.

By 2030, middle-class households are expected to spend $62 trillion — roughly 50% more than in 2020. Two in three middle-class members will be Asian.

The geographic concentration is critical for Booking. China's middle class exceeds 700 million. India's will add another 17.8% to close in on half a billion. In 2024, China and India together accounted for over half the global increase — 31 and 33 million newly middle-class people respectively. An Expedia survey found that 39% of mass-affluent Asian travelers would prioritize travel over purchasing a new car or upgrading electronics.

The travel connection is direct and well-documented: when people cross from poverty into the consumer class, one of the first expenditures they make is air travel. Colombia's El Dorado Airport saw flights double from 24.5 million in 2009 to 56.5 million in 2024 — a pattern repeating across emerging economies worldwide.

How Booking captures this trend

Dual-brand Asia strategy: Booking.com for international travelers and brand-conscious bookers, plus Agoda (fully owned) as a locally-tuned platform with deep Asian inventory, local payment methods, and language support across Southeast Asia, China, India, and Japan.

Merchant revenue model (66% of total, +26% YoY): By processing payments directly, Booking can offer WeChat Pay, UPI, GrabPay, and dozens of other local methods — removing the friction of international credit cards for first-time digital travelers.

Connected Trip: Bundles flights + hotels + attractions into a single transaction. Purpose-built for first-time international travelers who need guidance across multiple booking categories. Flights serve as a customer acquisition channel — 68 million airline tickets booked in FY2025 (+37%).

The experience economy

Travel has fundamentally shifted from a yearly vacation into a lifestyle constant. For younger generations, it's identity — not indulgence — and it's remarkably recession-resistant.

McKinsey reports that Millennials and Gen Z travel more frequently than older cohorts, averaging close to five trips per year compared to fewer than four among Gen X and Baby Boomers. They allocate approximately 29% of their income to travel — nearly a third of earnings directed at exactly the market Booking operates in. And this spending persists through downturns: over 55% say they plan to travel even during periods of austerity.

The behavior is fundamentally different from previous generations. Travel is no longer a single, fixed event requiring long-term planning. It's approached as a continuous process where searching, booking, changing plans, and cancelling are all parts of one ongoing cycle. A ticket purchase is often treated as an option that preserves flexibility, not a firm commitment.

"Gig-tripping" — planning travel around concerts, festivals, and sporting events — is creating entirely new booking occasions that didn't exist a decade ago.

During 2024 Carnival in Rio de Janeiro, nontravel spending increased by 156% compared to normal levels (Mastercard). Hotel demand in Manchester surged around the planned Oasis reunion. Sports drive massive travel: NFL games, the Olympic Games in Paris, Champions League matches. Gen Z is especially fond of anchoring travel around a central event — and Booking's Connected Trip (flights + hotels + experiences in one transaction) is purpose-built for this behavior.

Gen Z was the only consumer segment that spent more on travel year-over-year, averaging more than $11,000 per trip in 2024. Their global spending is projected to surge from $2.7 trillion in 2024 to $12.6 trillion by 2030. Meanwhile, 76% of millennials say they are likely to use an AI agent tool for travel recommendations — directly aligning with Booking's AI Trip Planner rollout across 10+ countries.

Gen Z travelers (18–28)

Travel intent: 55% plan to travel during holidays — tied with millennials for highest. 45% say "now is a good time to spend on leisure travel" — 12 points above non-Gen Z. The only generation increasing travel spend YoY, averaging $11,000+ per trip in 2024.

Behavior: Plans anchored around events ("gig-tripping"). Over 42% use TikTok to plan trips. 52% splurge on experiences while traveling. Heavy reliance on mobile apps — digital-native from day one. Prioritize experiences above material consumption.

Significance for Booking: Gen Z's global spending will reach $12.6T by 2030. As earning power grows, already-high travel frequency compounds with larger budgets. AI Trip Planner adoption rates are highest in this cohort. They represent the next 30+ years of booking volume.

Millennial travelers (29–44)

Travel intent: 55% plan travel even while cutting discretionary spending. Pioneered the experience economy — prioritizing moments over possessions. Travel is a regular lifestyle choice, not a rare fixed event. Will account for one-third of global social media shopping spend in 2025.

Behavior: Average ~5 trips/year. 47% splurge on experiences while traveling. 76% likely to use AI agent tools for travel recommendations. Most influenced by social media and peer content for destination selection. Increasingly blending work with leisure.

Significance for Booking: Now in peak earning years. As they start families, multigenerational trips expand booking size per transaction. Already the core volume engine for OTAs — and their preference for digital booking, flexible cancellation, and bundled trips aligns perfectly with Connected Trip.

Gen X travelers (45–60)

Travel intent: 39% plan to travel during holidays. Spending flat YoY — the "steady state" generation. Balancing career peak with family responsibilities, seeking convenience and value over novelty.

Behavior: 60% opt for all-inclusive family resorts. Increasingly blending work with leisure ("bleisure"). Budget-conscious but willing to pay for convenience. 33% of spending goes to groceries (practical orientation), but travel remains a top priority.

Significance for Booking: Gen X's family travel creates high-value multi-room bookings. Serves as the "connector" generation organizing multigenerational trips for both parents and children. Strong preference for hotel + resort properties with comprehensive amenities.

Baby Boomer travelers (61–79)

Travel intent: 41% cite travel as their #1 splurge category — the highest of any generation. 44% plan to increase vacation spending next year. 37% booking "gap year" style major trips post-pandemic. 56% willing to pay extra for luxury.

Behavior: $6,800/trip average — 70% more than millennials. 10.5 day average trips. 80% of all luxury travel spending. 74% want to bundle reservations. 27 days per year traveling. 84% book online. Europe #1 destination (41% of international trips).

Significance for Booking: The highest per-transaction revenue of any generation. Structurally insulated from rate hikes ($76T net worth, paid-off homes). 10-15 more years of peak travel ahead. And Europe — their preferred destination — is Booking's absolute stronghold.

The worldwide online travel industry reached an estimated $654 billion in 2024 and is forecast to exceed $1 trillion by 2030. Online channels will generate 76% of total travel revenue by 2030. In many emerging markets, the transition is from walk-in and phone reservations directly to mobile apps, skipping desktop entirely.

Mobile applications held 61% of the online accommodation booking market in 2025 and are advancing at a 13% CAGR through 2030. Mobile devices made up 70.5% of online travel traffic in 2024. Travel apps generated $629 billion in revenue — a 13% increase year-over-year. 72% of travelers now prefer booking online, versus only 12% who favor traditional agencies.

Booking.com is consistently among the most-downloaded travel apps globally with ~560 million monthly visits. Its mid-60% direct channel booking mix (unpaid traffic through app and direct web) is a critical cost advantage that widens as mobile usage grows — every direct booking avoids performance marketing spend.

The number of US digital nomads has increased from 7.3 million in 2019 to 18.1 million in 2025 — a 148% increase. Globally, estimates put the count at 40–60 million. MBO Partners confirms digital nomadism now comprises approximately 12% of the US workforce.

What makes this relevant for Booking is the type of travel generated. Nomads visited an average of 6.6 locations in 2024, spending 5.7 weeks per location. That's extended-stay travel requiring long-duration accommodations — mapping perfectly to Booking's 8.6 million alternative listings.

The trend is being institutionalized: 64+ countries offer digital nomad visas, companies are adopting nomad-friendly policies, and Gen Z is entering the workforce expecting location independence. "Bleisure" travel — extending business trips — means every corporate trip is a potential additional OTA booking.

How Booking captures this trend

8.6 million alternative accommodation listings — apartments, villas, unique stays — representing 36% of total room nights and growing at ~16% CAGR. This inventory serves extended-stay travelers who need kitchens, workspaces, and residential amenities.

Utilization advantage: While Airbnb has ~7M unique listings, Booking's utilization is materially higher — it booked an estimated 114 million alternative lodging nights in Q2 2025 alone, approaching 85% of Airbnb's total. Booking dominates European alternative accommodations with 48% market share.

Baby boomers spend $157 billion annually on travel in the US alone. They make up nearly a third of all leisure travelers, spending $6,600–$6,800 per trip — that's 20–50% more than Gen X or millennials. 23% spend more than $6,000 per trip, and they contribute a staggering 80% of all luxury travel spending globally.

The accommodation premium is striking: boomers spend over double what millennials do on hotels ($1,540 vs. $675 per trip). They travel 27 days per year on average, with trips lasting 10.5 days. Europe is their #1 international destination at 41% — precisely Booking.com's strongest market.

Baby boomers hold a record $76 trillion in net worth. Most have paid-off homes and carry less consumer debt than any other generation. Their travel budgets are structurally insulated from rate hikes and recessions.

The digital-averse stereotype is outdated: 84% use online services to book travel. And their behavior maps perfectly to Booking's platform: 74% prefer to bundle reservations (Connected Trip), 79% comparison-shop for deals (OTA transparency), and 66% prefer hotels for international travel (Booking's core inventory). 95% want to know they have the best possible deal before booking.

Why boomer travel is recession-resistant Key insight

Boomers are structurally insulated from economic pressures hitting younger travelers. Most have locked in mortgage rates or paid off homes entirely. They aren't burdened with student loans. They carry less consumer debt than any other generation.

This creates a structural floor under travel demand that the market may be underpricing at 16x forward earnings.

The youngest boomers are only 61–62. This wave has 10–15 years of peak travel spending ahead. 44% plan to increase vacation spending next year. Post-pandemic, 37% booked "gap year" style major trips. The urgency of "someday is now" is a permanent psychological shift.

The multigenerational multiplier Growth vector

▼

Boomers increasingly fund and organize multigenerational trips, pulling adult children and grandchildren into the ecosystem. A villa in Tuscany for the whole family is a high-value, multi-room booking — often in alternative accommodations. These trips are longer, more expensive, and involve multiple service categories.

81% travel with a spouse/partner, and "skip-generation" trips (grandparents + grandchildren) are a fast-growing sub-category. Each multigenerational trip represents the highest per-transaction revenue density on the platform.

Asia-Pacific carriers carried 390 million passengers in 2025, up 9.4%. Demand rose 11% by revenue passenger kilometers, and load factors hit a record 82.2%. IATA forecasts APAC growth at 7.9% — the highest globally.

The penetration gap tells the real story. India: 0.1 yearly air trips per capita vs China's 0.5 and the US's 2.1. Even by 2043, India reaches only 0.4. India will surpass China in traffic growth (10.5% vs 8.9% in 2026). China targets 810M passenger trips in 2026.

Airbus forecasts 19,560 new aircraft deliveries in APAC over 20 years — 46% of global demand. Southeast Asian markets (Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines) are structurally shifting from rail/bus to air.

China: 810M passenger target for 2026 Recovery

▼

Chinese domestic capacity is now 14% above 2019 levels. The fleet has grown to 4,394 registered transport aircraft linking 263 cities in 90 countries. International traffic is recovering — up 21.6% YoY — but still only at ~90% of pre-pandemic levels. The outbound recovery is where the real upside lies: traffic to Latin America surged 109%, Africa +39%, Central Asia +59%.

Booking's opening: Trip.com faces an anti-monopoly investigation (launched Jan 2026), creating a window for Booking/Agoda to gain share without the same regulatory scrutiny.

India: 0.1 trips per capita — 20x the US runway Explosive

▼

India operates 159 airports with 50 more planned. Airlines have ordered 1,700+ new planes. Growth rate will hit 10.5% in 2026, surpassing China. But the real story is penetration: 0.1 trips per capita means India has barely begun. Even after two decades of growth, India in 2043 will still be at less than one-fifth of current US levels.

Booking's play: Merchant revenue with UPI integration removes the credit card barrier. India's mobile-first consumers skip desktop entirely.

Southeast Asia: the low-cost carrier revolution Structural shift

▼

Southeast Asia leads APAC growth. Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines are shifting from rail/bus/ferry to air travel on domestic and short-haul routes. Low-cost carriers (AirAsia, VietJet, Lion Air) have democratized flying for hundreds of millions of first-time passengers.

Booking's play: Agoda has deep local inventory across Southeast Asia with GrabPay, GoPay, and regional wallet integrations. The brand is significantly stronger than Expedia in APAC. Every new budget route creates accommodation demand at the destination.

The valuation disconnect

Booking captures roughly $27 billion of an $11.7 trillion global travel market — barely 2–3% of a market growing at 7%+ annually with six secular tailwinds compounding underneath.

The stock trades at ~16x forward earnings with a PEG ratio under 1.0, an FCF yield of 6.3%, and total shareholder yield above 5%. The market is pricing cyclical risk and AI disruption fear, while largely ignoring the underlying demand runway is longer and wider than it's ever been.

The middle class doesn't shrink back. Gen Z doesn't stop valuing experiences. Remote work doesn't fully return to the office. Boomers don't un-retire. Asia doesn't stop building airports. These are decade-long structural shifts — and Booking is positioned at their convergence.
